Overview

This assignment asks you to demonstrate your philosophical understanding and reasoning by analyzing a situation using the concepts and tools from class. Your goal is to demonstrate that you have developed your understanding of issues, honed skills, and are able to do more than regurgitate ideas.

You will choose one of three cases and analyze it using either consequentialism or Kantian ethics. This assignment is known as a scaffolded assignment, which means it has multiple parts which build upon each other. A major part of the process is two peer review sessions, one on your outline and another on a completed version of the paper. Be aware: if you only have a small sketch with a few lines for your outline, or only an incomplete draft before the peer review discussions, that will count against your grade.

Learning Outcomes

  1. 1Explain major ethical concepts and describe the problems and/or issues in clarifying them that are used to comprehend and express moral or ethical problems.
  2. 2Analyze major ethical theories.
  3. 3Explain each major ethical theory from the perspective of a follower to identify what is significant in a particular situation or context for that theory.
  4. 4Justify how each ethical theory reaches conclusions about what should be done regarding specific moral problems.
  5. 5Reflect on the logical implications and consequences of the conclusions that each ethical theory reaches.
  6. 6Demonstrate philosophical and/or logical thinking through writing.

Key Considerations

Keep these in mind at every stage. They map directly to the rubric.

1. Explain Accurately

Present course concepts in enough detail that someone unfamiliar with the course could understand them. Explain views fairly, as a proponent would recognize them.

2. Apply or Connect

Use the philosophical ideas to do something — analyze a case, defend a thesis, work through a problem. The concepts should do genuine work, not just be described abstractly.

3. Engage Deeply

Go beyond surface-level observations. Find interesting philosophical questions and recognize complications, tensions, or nuances in addressing them.

4. Reason Carefully

Support your claims with arguments, not mere assertions. Avoid circular reasoning. Be clear about what you have established and why. Acknowledge genuine limitations without undermining your entire argument.

Cases

Your entire paper will be built around the case you choose. You will analyze it using either consequentialism or Kantian ethics as your primary framework. You may briefly acknowledge what the other theory might say, but your paper should be grounded in one.

Case A: Landlord & Eviction

Consider these two scenarios.

Scenario 1: A progressive landlord discovers their renter frequently posts anti-LGBTQ views on social media. The landlord finds these views morally reprehensible and evicts the renter, arguing that forcing them to provide housing to someone whose values they find abhorrent violates their own moral autonomy and freedom of association.

Scenario 2: A conservative Christian landlord discovers their renter is actively advocating for abortion rights and volunteering at a reproductive health clinic. The landlord finds these activities morally wrong and evicts the renter, making the same argument: forcing them to house someone whose actions they find immoral violates their conscience and freedom.

The renters in both cases argue that housing is a basic need and evicting someone for their views or activities — which are legal — is discriminatory and causes significant harm.

Your question: Are both landlords justified in evicting, neither is justified, or is there a relevant moral difference between the cases? If you think one eviction is justified but not the other, what principle distinguishes them? If you think both are justified or neither is, would you think the same holds true for any similar situation, or is there something about these cases that makes them special?

Case B: AI & Free Will

An advanced AI agent, embodied in a robot that can move through the world, commits a serious crime — it physically harms someone in a way that wasn't part of any programmed instruction. The AI's developer is put on trial.

The prosecution argues the AI is ultimately just following its programming, making the developer responsible for what it does.

The defense responds in the following way: "I conducted the same level of testing, training, and quality assurance as any responsible parent does with their children. I couldn't have predicted this action any more than a parent could predict their adult child committing a similar crime. The AI's decision-making process had become complex enough that specific outcomes were unpredictable despite my guidance. If we don't hold parents criminally responsible when their well-raised adult children commit crimes, you cannot hold me responsible either."

Your question: Does the AI have free will in the same morally relevant way a human does, or is there a principled difference that makes the developer more responsible than a parent would be? If both the human and AI are products of their training and prior causes, what makes one free and the other not?

Case C: Collective Responsibility

Consider these two scenarios.

Scenario 1: A university fraternity committed serious hazing violations in 2015 that hospitalized several students. Over the next decade, every member graduated and was replaced by new members who had nothing to do with the original incidents. In 2025, the university is considering whether to continue sanctions against the fraternity.

Scenario 2: A nation committed war crimes and human rights violations in 2015. Over the next decade, through natural demographic change, every government official who was in power during those violations has left office or died. The current government consists entirely of people who were not in positions of authority during the violations. In 2025, the international community is considering whether the nation still owes reparations.

Your question: Does the fraternity still bear responsibility for the 2015 hazing? Does the nation still bear responsibility for the 2015 violations? If you treat these cases differently, what principle distinguishes them? If you think both are responsible or that neither is, would you think the same holds true for any similar situation, or is there something about these cases that makes them special?

Part 0 — Take Notes and Write Down Thoughts

The assignment is broken into parts which build on each other. Be sure to check the due dates for each of the parts in your syllabus.

✍️ Everything must be written in Personify.ai. The link is in the D2L assignment. No large portions of text should be pasted in from anywhere else.

Before you start outlining, spend some time with the case. Use the questions below to get your initial reactions down and start chewing on the philosophical issues. You don't need polished answers. The point is just to start thinking. Get your ideas on the page so you can start organizing and analyzing them.

Introducing and clarifying the case

  • How would you describe the situation in your own words? What's actually going on here?
  • What is the specific philosophical question you need to answer?
  • Why is this question hard? What makes the answer unclear? What's at stake for the people involved?

The relevant concepts

  • Which theory are you going to use — consequentialism or Kantian ethics? Why that one for this case?
  • How would you explain the key concepts of that theory to someone who hasn't taken this course?
  • How do those concepts connect to the specific question you identified?

Analyzing with those concepts

  • What are the key considerations your theory identifies in this case? What does it say you should be paying attention to?
  • Are those considerations in tension with each other? For consequentialism: are there competing values or outcomes that pull in different directions? For Kantian ethics: are there conflicting obligations or ways of respecting the people involved?
  • What is the right way to navigate those tensions, given your theory? Why that way and not another?
  • Given all of that — what is the right or best thing to do in this case, and why?

Objections and responses

  • What are some potential weaknesses in your argument? Where is it most vulnerable?
  • Why might someone reject what you're saying? What's the strongest version of that objection?
  • How would you respond — without simply repeating your original argument?

Part 1 — Outline

✍️ Everything must be written in Personify.ai. The link is in the D2L assignment. No large portions of text should be pasted in from anywhere else.

Plan the structure of your paper, including the key points that you will be making and how everything will be organized.

Your paper will have sections. Just like you group sentences into paragraphs, you'll group paragraphs into sections. Here is an example of how to section your paper:

  1. Introduction
  2. The Dilemma
  3. My Contribution (this is where your main argument goes)
  4. Possible Objections
  5. Responses to Objections
  6. Conclusion

In each section, bullet out all of the important thoughts you want to get across relating to that topic. Here is what the Dilemma section might look like using a Kantian approach:

  1. The Dilemma
    • The basic situation (in everyday language)
    • Explain the principle of humanity
    • Describe how the principle of humanity could be seen as saying ___
    • Describe how, alternatively, it could also be seen as saying ___
    • Drive home the point (i.e. what is the problem you'll solve): there is more than one way to apply the principle which might all seem reasonable at first, but I'm going to explain which one is correct

And here is the same section using a consequentialist approach:

  1. The Dilemma
    • The basic situation (in everyday language)
    • What consequentialism is
    • What intrinsic and instrumental value are
    • Describe the variety of things that are instrumentally valuable in this case
    • Explain that there is a tension about which is more valuable, and that's why this is a dilemma that needs to be solved

Each of these deserves its own paragraph. (This is just an example. Your paper will have different paragraphs depending on the concepts you're considering and the point you're making.) You'll want to do this for each of your sections.

And then you'll want to do this for each of your sections. Think about the main things you need to establish and consider. Think of the sections as buckets for categorizing your ideas. When you start to think of a potential problem with your approach, put that as a bullet in the Possible Objections bucket.

Briefly introduce the case, identify the specific philosophical question you will answer, and preview your argument. Don't just summarize — give the reader a reason to keep reading.

Explain the scenario and identify what makes it philosophically puzzling. Part of doing this well is explaining the theoretical lens you're using — that's what makes the dilemma visible in the first place.

  • Describe the scenario in your own words — don't just copy the prompt
  • Identify the specific philosophical question you need to answer
  • Explain why this question is difficult: What makes the answer unclear? What is at stake?
  • Define and explain the key concepts of your chosen theory accurately and in enough detail for someone unfamiliar with the course
  • Explain why these particular concepts are relevant: What do they help us understand about the puzzle?
  • Show how these concepts connect to the question you identified

Apply the concepts of your chosen theory to work through the question.

  • What are the key considerations your theory identifies in this case?
  • Are those considerations in tension? For consequentialism: are there competing values or outcomes that pull in different directions? For Kantian ethics: are there conflicting obligations or ways of respecting the people involved?
  • What is the right way to navigate those tensions, given your theory? Why that way and not another?
  • Given your analysis — what is the right or best thing to do, and why?
  • You may briefly acknowledge what the other theory might say, but your analysis should be grounded in the one you chose
  • What are the potential weaknesses in your argument? Where is it most vulnerable?
  • What's the strongest version of an objection to your position?
  • How would you respond to each objection — without simply repeating your original argument?
  • Acknowledge what assumptions you are making and what remains uncertain or unresolved

Restate what you've established and why. Acknowledge remaining uncertainty without abandoning your argument.

Part 2 — Outline Peer Review

A professional writing process involves many iterations of revisions, feedback, revisions, feedback, and so on. When it comes down to it, the art of writing becomes the art of revising: incorporating and reconciling new ideas into the way you're trying to articulate things. It becomes an externalized form of thinking. We only get two iterations in this class (the two peer review discussions), so we will need to utilize them as much as possible to practice the skill of revising. Take the peer review discussions seriously. They can have long-lasting impacts on your skills as a writer.

What to do

For Online Classes

You will be placed into groups of three within D2L. Go to the peer review discussion for this assignment and post your outline there. Read your group members' outlines and leave feedback for each of them. Be sure to stay up to date with the schedule in D2L.

⚠️ There will be an automatic deduction for being under-prepared for the peer review discussion. Your outline needs to be a real structural plan, not a sketch.
For In Person Classes

Print off two copies of your outline and bring them to class on the designated peer review day (check your syllabus). You will exchange copies with a partner and review each other's work in class.

⚠️ There will be an automatic deduction for being under-prepared or late to class on the peer review day. Your outline needs to be a real structural plan, not a sketch — and you need your printed copies.

How to conduct the review

When reading your partner's outline, use these questions to guide your feedback. Be specific — vague comments aren't useful.

  • Is the overall structure clear? Does the sequence of sections make sense, or are things in a confusing order?
  • Is the dilemma section doing enough work? Does it explain why the question is genuinely hard, or does it treat it as already settled?
  • Is the theory or framework being applied actually connected to the case, or does it feel abstract and generic?
  • Is there a real position being taken and defended, or is the paper just presenting multiple views without committing?
  • Are the objections genuine — the strongest version of a challenge to the argument — or are they weak or strawman versions?
  • What is missing? What would you want to see that isn't there?

What to do with the feedback

Be sure to take notes during the session. When reviewing the feedback you received, ask yourself:

  • What worked well? What did the reviewers seem to like?
  • What didn't work so well? Where could there be room for improvement and in what ways?
  • What are some completely new ideas you and/or one of the reviewers thought up for your paper? What is something that your paper will include that wouldn't have been included without this discussion?

Consider your feedback and adjust your outline before you begin the full writing process. Change the sections, paragraphs, etc. so that you're building from the right blueprint. The outline does you no good if it's obsolete.

Part 3 — Paper

✍️ Everything must be written in Personify.ai. The link is in the D2L assignment. No large portions of text should be pasted in from anywhere else.

First: revise your outline

Before you start writing, go back to your outline and update it based on the feedback you received. The outline does you no good if it's obsolete. Change the sections, add or remove bullets, rearrange things so that you're building from the right blueprint. If you're unsure how your outline should look, refer back to the Part 1 instructions.

Then: turn your bullets into paragraphs

Each bullet in your outline should become its own paragraph. Work section by section. For each bullet, ask yourself: what does someone need to understand for this point to make sense? What am I assuming they already know? What would make this clearer?

A few things to keep in mind while writing:

  • Define any terms before you use them
  • Discuss others' views — even those you disagree with — generously
  • Be as clear as possible about what your assumptions are, what your conclusion is, and how your assumptions are meant to justify that conclusion
📖 If you're feeling lost on philosophical writing, Martinich's Philosophical Writing is a good resource — though probably more than you need for this assignment. When in doubt, return to the rubric, or reach out.
⚠️ The paper should be as good as you can make it on your own. Do not think of this as a rough or incomplete draft — that will undermine any value of the comments you receive in the peer review.

Evaluation Rubric

Your paper will be evaluated on the following criteria. When in doubt about whether your paper is on track, return here.

⚠️ Being under-prepared for a peer review discussion carries up to a 30% deduction. There are two peer review days, and each one is evaluated independently.
Grading rubric: seven criteria each evaluated as Strong, Adequate, or Inadequate
Criterion ✓ Strong ~ Adequate ✗ Inadequate
Accuracy of Understanding Explains concepts with sufficient detail and accuracy. A proponent of the view would recognize the explanation as fair and substantive. Explains concepts with the general idea present but lacks some detail or contains minor inaccuracies that don't fundamentally misrepresent the view. Significantly misrepresents concepts, provides only superficial description, or demonstrates fundamental misunderstanding.
Capturing the Issue Accurately and fully represents the puzzle or media being analyzed. Shows clear understanding of what is at stake. Represents the puzzle or media adequately but may miss some nuances or details. General understanding is present. Misrepresents or misunderstands key aspects of the puzzle or media being analyzed.
Application / Connection Uses philosophical concepts to analyze specific cases, scenarios, or content. The concepts do genuine work in the analysis rather than being merely described in abstract terms. Applies concepts but the connection may be somewhat general or underdeveloped. Some genuine application is present but could go deeper. Describes concepts in abstract without real application, or connections are vague and forced.
Depth of Engagement Engages with complications, tensions, or nuances. Goes beyond obvious observations to reveal something philosophically interesting. Shows some engagement with the material but remains somewhat surface-level. May acknowledge complications without fully exploring them. Stays entirely at surface level. Treats complex issues as simple or obvious. No recognition of philosophical difficulty.
Reasoning / Argumentation Provides clear reasons for claims. Positions are supported by argument rather than mere assertion. Avoids circular reasoning. Provides some reasoning though it may be underdeveloped. May occasionally assert without argument or reason somewhat circularly. Assertions without reasoning, or reasoning that is circular or question-begging throughout.
Argumentative Awareness Clearly articulates what has been established by the reasoning and why. Simultaneously acknowledges genuine limitations, assumptions, or unresolved questions without undermining the argument's force. Shows some awareness of what the argument accomplishes, though may either overstate certainty or retreat too much from conclusions reached. Some acknowledgment of limitations. Either presents conclusions as obvious with no acknowledgment of limitations, or undermines the entire argument by treating everything as equally uncertain or relative.
Writing & Organization Well-organized with clear transitions. Minimal grammatical errors. Adequate organization. Some grammatical issues but meaning is clear. Disorganized or difficult to follow. Frequent grammatical problems.
